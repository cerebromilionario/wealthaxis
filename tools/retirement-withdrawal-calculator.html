Retirement Withdrawal FAQs

How long should my retirement savings last?

Financial planners typically plan for retirement assets to last 30+ years. If you retire at 65, planning to age 95 is prudent (30 years). For early retirees (45-55), plan for 40-50 years. This calculator shows exactly how long your specific portfolio will last given your withdrawal amount and expected returns — and lets you identify the sustainable withdrawal level.

What is the safe withdrawal rate?

The 4% rule (from the 1994 Trinity Study) suggests withdrawing 4% of your initial portfolio in year 1, then adjusting for inflation annually, has historically sustained a 30-year retirement with a ~95% success rate using a 60/40 stock/bond portfolio. For longer retirements (40+ years), 3.5% is more conservative. For shorter retirements or higher stock allocations, 4.5-5% may be sustainable. This calculator uses your specific inputs to model the actual trajectory.

Does Social Security affect how much I can withdraw?

Significantly. Social Security income reduces the burden on your portfolio — every $1,000/month in SS income is like having an extra $300,000 in your portfolio (at a 4% withdrawal rate). Enter your expected Social Security or pension income in the "Additional Income" field to see how it extends your portfolio's longevity. The full retirement age for Social Security is 67 for those born after 1960, but benefits can be claimed as early as 62 (at a reduced rate) or as late as 70 (at a higher rate).

What investment return should I use in retirement?

In retirement, most financial planners recommend a more conservative portfolio than during accumulation. A 60/40 stock/bond portfolio has historically returned 7-8% nominal. Adjusted for 3% inflation, that's a 4-5% real return. Use 6-7% nominal (3-4% real) for a moderate estimate. Using 5-6% nominal is more conservative and accounts for potentially lower future returns. Never assume 10%+ returns in retirement planning.
