Frequently Asked Questions

What is the historical US inflation rate?

The US average annual inflation rate from 1913 to 2024 has been approximately 3.2%. However, inflation is highly variable — it was nearly 0% during the Great Depression, reached 14% in 1980, stayed below 2% from 2012-2021, then spiked to 9.1% in June 2022 before declining. For long-term planning, 3% is a reasonable assumption, though current conditions may warrant 3.5-4%.

How does inflation affect investments?

Inflation erodes real returns. A 10% nominal investment return with 3% inflation equals a 7% real return. When inflation spikes, it particularly damages fixed-income investments like bonds (whose fixed payments lose value in real terms), cash savings, and assets with no natural inflation link. Best inflation hedges: stocks (companies can raise prices), real estate (property values and rents tend to rise with inflation), TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities), commodities, and I-Bonds.

What investments beat inflation historically?

Over long periods, US stocks have beaten inflation by ~7% annually (10% nominal minus 3% inflation). Real estate has beaten inflation by ~1-2% in price appreciation, plus rental income. Bonds have barely kept pace or slightly lagged inflation over very long periods. Gold has maintained purchasing power over centuries but provides no income. Cash savings almost always lose purchasing power over time — the silent tax of inflation is the core reason to invest rather than hold cash.
