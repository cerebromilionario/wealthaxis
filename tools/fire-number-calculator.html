FIRE Number FAQs

What is the 4% rule?

The 4% rule (from the 1994 Trinity Study) states that a retiree can withdraw 4% of their initial portfolio value annually, adjusted for inflation, and have a very high probability (historically ~95%) of the portfolio lasting 30 years. Your FIRE number = Annual expenses ÷ 0.04 = Annual expenses × 25.

Is 4% safe for a 50-year retirement?

The original Trinity Study was designed for 30-year retirements. For early retirees facing 40-50 year retirements, a 3.5% or 3.3% withdrawal rate provides better protection against sequence-of-returns risk. Adjust the Safe Withdrawal Rate slider to 3.5% for a more conservative FIRE number if you're planning a very long retirement.

What is Lean FIRE vs Fat FIRE?

Lean FIRE means retiring on a frugal budget (~$40,000/year), requiring a smaller portfolio (~$1M) but demanding significant lifestyle minimalism. Fat FIRE means retiring with $120,000+ in annual spending, requiring $3M+ but allowing a comfortable lifestyle. Most people target somewhere in between — "regular" FIRE on $60,000-$80,000/year.

Does Social Security affect my FIRE number?

Yes significantly. If you plan to collect Social Security benefits at 62-67, that income reduces how much your portfolio needs to provide. For example, if SS provides $20,000/year and your expenses are $60,000, your portfolio only needs to cover $40,000 — reducing your FIRE number from $1,500,000 to $1,000,000. This calculator uses portfolio-only funding; subtract any expected Social Security from your annual expenses for a more accurate FIRE number.
