How Does Compound Interest Work?

What is compound interest?

Compound interest is interest calculated on both your initial principal and the accumulated interest from previous periods. Unlike simple interest (which only earns on the principal), compound interest earns interest on interest — creating exponential growth over time. Einstein is often (apocryphally) credited with calling it the "eighth wonder of the world."

How does compounding frequency affect returns?

More frequent compounding means slightly higher effective returns. Daily compounding yields marginally more than annual compounding at the same stated rate. The difference becomes significant with higher interest rates and longer time horizons. For most investment accounts (index funds, ETFs), compounding is effectively continuous as prices adjust daily.

What is the Rule of 72?

Divide 72 by your annual interest rate to estimate years to double your money. At 8% return, 72 ÷ 8 = 9 years to double. At 6%, it takes 12 years. This mental shortcut is accurate within 1-2% for typical investment return ranges.

What average return should I use?

The US stock market (S&P 500) has historically returned approximately 10% nominal and 7% real (inflation-adjusted) annually over long periods. For a conservative estimate, use 6-7%. For moderate projections use 8%, and for historical averages use 10%. Never assume returns are guaranteed — actual results vary significantly year to year.

Why does starting early matter so much?

The later years of a long compound interest journey contribute the most growth in absolute dollar terms. An investor who starts at 25 vs. 35 doesn't just earn 10 more years of returns — those early 10 years compound for the entire remaining period and can account for more than half the final portfolio value. Time is the single most valuable input in this calculator.
