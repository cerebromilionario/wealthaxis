Why Fund Fees Matter So Much

What is an expense ratio?

An expense ratio is the annual fee a mutual fund or ETF charges, expressed as a percentage of assets. A 1% expense ratio on a $100,000 portfolio costs $1,000/year — but because that $1,000 can't compound, the actual long-term cost is vastly higher. Low-cost index ETFs like VOO charge 0.03%, while actively managed funds often charge 0.5-1.5%.

Are there other fees beyond expense ratios?

Yes: load fees (sales commissions of 3-5% charged at purchase or redemption), 12b-1 fees (marketing fees embedded in the expense ratio), trading commissions (largely eliminated at major brokerages in 2020), and advisory fees (typically 0.25-1% annually for financial advisors or robo-advisors). Total cost of ownership can exceed 2% annually when all fees are included.

What expense ratio should I target?

Broad market index ETFs from Vanguard, Fidelity, and Schwab offer expense ratios of 0.03-0.10% — essentially free. As a rule of thumb: under 0.10% is excellent, 0.10-0.30% is acceptable, 0.30-0.75% requires strong justification, and above 0.75% is very difficult to justify given the evidence that active management rarely outperforms net of fees.
