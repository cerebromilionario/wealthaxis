Understanding ROI and CAGR

What is ROI?

Return on Investment (ROI) is the percentage gain or loss on an investment relative to its cost. Formula: ROI = (Final Value − Initial Investment + Income − Fees) ÷ Initial Investment × 100. A 50% ROI means you earned 50 cents for every dollar invested. ROI doesn't account for time — a 50% return in 1 year is dramatically better than 50% over 10 years.

What is CAGR and why does it matter?

Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) is the annualized rate of return that accounts for compounding over the holding period. It's the most useful metric for comparing investments held over different timeframes. Formula: CAGR = (Final Value / Initial Value)^(1/years) − 1. A 10% CAGR over 10 years produces the same result as 10% compounded annually — regardless of the actual year-by-year fluctuations.

What's a good ROI for an investment?

Context is everything. The S&P 500 has historically returned ~10% annually. A "good" ROI depends on the asset class, risk level, and time period. For stocks, anything consistently above 8-10% annually is exceptional. For real estate, 6-12% annualized total returns (including rental income and appreciation) is typical for well-selected properties. For bonds, 4-6% is reasonable. Always compare ROI against the risk-free rate (Treasury yields) and inflation.

Why does the after-tax return matter?

Pre-tax returns are misleading for comparing investments held in different account types. A 10% return in a taxable account for a 37% bracket investor nets only 6.3% after federal taxes. The same investment in a Roth IRA keeps the full 10%. Always evaluate investments on an after-tax basis to compare accurately across account types.
