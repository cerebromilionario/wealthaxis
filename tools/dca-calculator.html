DCA vs. Lump Sum Explained

What is Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA)?

DCA means investing a fixed amount at regular intervals — e.g., $1,000/month for 12 months — instead of investing a large sum all at once. The key benefit: you buy more shares when prices are low and fewer when prices are high, potentially lowering your average cost basis over time. It's also the natural way most people invest through regular paycheck contributions.

Does lump sum really outperform DCA?

According to Vanguard research analyzing US, UK, and Australian markets since 1926, lump sum investing outperforms 12-month DCA approximately 68% of the time, with an average advantage of 2.3% in the first year. The reason: markets trend upward more often than downward, so being fully invested sooner captures more expected return. However, in the ~32% of cases where markets decline after your investment, DCA would have been superior.

When should I choose DCA over lump sum?

DCA makes more sense when: (1) you're emotionally risk-averse and couldn't handle an immediate large loss without panic-selling, (2) markets appear historically overvalued, (3) the lump sum represents a one-time windfall you can't replace, or (4) you're investing near a retirement date with a shorter recovery horizon. For most regular paycheck investors, monthly contributions are DCA by default — the debate only applies when you receive a windfall.

What's the optimal DCA period?

Research suggests 6-12 months is optimal if you choose DCA. Shorter periods capture more lump-sum-like returns while still providing some psychological smoothing. Longer DCA periods (24+ months) dramatically increase opportunity cost in upward-trending markets. If you DCA over more than 12 months, you're almost certainly leaving significant returns on the table.
