Tax-Loss Harvesting: How to Turn Losing Investments Into Tax Savings

January 30, 202611 min read

By WealthAxis Editorial Team

Our editorial team researches personal finance and investing topics using primary sources, regulatory guidance, and long-term market evidence to deliver clear, practical analysis.

Every investor has investments that lose money — at least temporarily. Most investors simply hold through the loss or sell in frustration. But sophisticated investors use market downturns as a tax optimization opportunity through a strategy called tax-loss harvesting. Done correctly, it can add 0.5% to 1.5% per year to your after-tax returns — essentially for free, with no change in your market exposure or long-term investment strategy.

The core idea: Sell an investment that has declined in value to realize a capital loss, use that loss to offset capital gains (or ordinary income), then immediately reinvest the proceeds in a similar (but not identical) investment so you maintain your market exposure. You've locked in a tax benefit without meaningfully altering your portfolio.

How Capital Gains and Losses Work

When you sell an investment for more than you paid, you realize a capital gain — which is taxable. When you sell for less than you paid, you realize a capital loss — which can offset gains. The tax treatment depends on your holding period:

  • Short-term gains/losses: Assets held less than 1 year. Short-term gains are taxed at ordinary income rates (up to 37%).
  • Long-term gains/losses: Assets held more than 1 year. Long-term gains are taxed at preferential rates: 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income.

Capital losses first offset capital gains of the same type (short offsets short, long offsets long). If losses exceed gains, you can deduct up to $3,000 of net capital losses against ordinary income per year. Any remaining losses carry forward indefinitely to future tax years.

A Step-by-Step Tax-Loss Harvesting Example

Here's a concrete example to illustrate the strategy:

Suppose you bought $50,000 of VTI (Vanguard Total Market ETF) in January. By October, it's worth $42,000 — a $8,000 unrealized loss. You also sold some appreciated real estate earlier in the year, generating $15,000 in long-term capital gains.

Without harvesting: You owe taxes on $15,000 in gains. At the 15% long-term capital gains rate, that's $2,250 in taxes due.

With harvesting: You sell your VTI position, realizing the $8,000 loss. You simultaneously buy a similar but not identical fund — say, ITOT (iShares Core S&P Total US Stock Market ETF) — with the proceeds. Your net capital gain is now $15,000 - $8,000 = $7,000. You owe taxes on only $7,000, saving $1,200 in taxes immediately.

Your market exposure is essentially unchanged (both VTI and ITOT track broad US stock markets). You've reduced your tax bill by $1,200 while maintaining your investment position. That $1,200 stays invested and compounds.

The Wash-Sale Rule: The Critical Constraint

The IRS isn't naive — they anticipated the tax-loss harvesting strategy and created the wash-sale rule to prevent pure tax arbitrage. The wash-sale rule disallows a capital loss deduction if you buy a "substantially identical" security within 30 days before or after the sale — creating a 61-day window in total.

What counts as "substantially identical"? The IRS hasn't published an exhaustive definition, which creates some gray areas, but the practical guidance is:

  • Clearly wash sales: Selling VTI and buying VTI back within 30 days. Selling an individual stock and buying it back. Selling SPY and buying an S&P 500 futures contract.
  • Generally not wash sales: Selling VTI (total US market) and buying ITOT (also total US market but different index and provider). The IRS has not formally declared broad index ETFs tracking similar (but different) indexes as substantially identical. Most tax professionals consider this an acceptable substitution.
  • Gray areas: Selling VOO (S&P 500) and buying IVV (also S&P 500) — these track the same index and are more likely to be treated as substantially identical. Better to use total market as a substitute for S&P 500.

The 30-day window applies to all accounts — your IRA, your spouse's accounts, and your taxable accounts are all combined for wash-sale purposes. This is a commonly missed detail that invalidates harvesting for many investors.

Common Swap Pairs for Tax-Loss Harvesting

Here are well-established fund swap pairs that maintain market exposure while avoiding wash-sale risk:

  • VTI → ITOT or SCHB (US total market, different indexes)
  • VXUS → IXUS or SCHF+SCHE (international, different providers)
  • BND → AGG or SCHZ (total bond, different indexes)
  • QQQ → ONEQ or XLK (tech-heavy, different approaches)
  • Individual stock → Sector ETF (specific stock to sector fund)

After 31 days, you can swap back to your original fund if desired, though many investors simply keep the replacement fund permanently — the differences between similar broad index ETFs are negligible for long-term investors.

When Tax-Loss Harvesting Is Most Valuable

Not all investors benefit equally from TLH. The strategy is most valuable when:

  • You're in a high tax bracket: The higher your capital gains tax rate, the more valuable a deductible loss. For investors in the 0% capital gains bracket (taxable income under ~$47,000 for single filers in 2026), TLH provides little benefit.
  • You have capital gains to offset: Harvesting losses is most immediately useful when you have realized gains in the same year from property sales, business sales, or other investment dispositions.
  • During market corrections: Significant drawdowns create significant harvesting opportunities. The March 2020 COVID crash created massive TLH opportunities for investors who acted quickly.
  • Early in your investing career: Losses harvested early create a tax cost basis that carries forward and reduces taxes on future gains — a benefit that compounds over decades.

The Long-Term Value: Deferral, Not Elimination

An important nuance: tax-loss harvesting typically defers taxes rather than eliminates them. When you sell at a loss and buy a replacement fund, your cost basis in the new fund is the price you paid after the swap. If the replacement fund subsequently rises, you'll eventually owe taxes on those gains when you sell.

The benefit is the time value of money: $1,200 saved in taxes today, invested and compounding at 8% for 20 years, grows to approximately $5,600. You eventually pay taxes on that amount, but you've used the government's money for 20 years as an interest-free loan.

In some cases, tax-loss harvesting can be truly permanent: if you hold the replacement fund until death, your heirs receive a stepped-up cost basis and the deferred gain is never taxed under current law. This makes TLH even more valuable for estate planning purposes.

Automated Tax-Loss Harvesting: Robo-Advisors

For investors who don't want to manually monitor and execute harvesting opportunities, robo-advisors like Betterment and Wealthfront offer automated daily tax-loss harvesting as a core feature. Their algorithms scan portfolios daily for harvesting opportunities and execute swaps automatically.

Wealthfront claims their automated TLH adds approximately 1.1% per year in after-tax returns for taxable portfolios — though real-world results vary based on tax bracket, portfolio size, and market volatility. The automation eliminates the need to monitor prices and execute trades yourself, which is particularly valuable during volatile periods when opportunities arise rapidly.

The tradeoff: robo-advisors charge 0.25% in management fees, which offsets some of the TLH benefit. For portfolios under $100,000, the math may not favor robo-advisors. For $500,000+ portfolios with significant capital gains exposure, the net benefit is often positive.

Practical Implementation for DIY Investors

If you prefer self-directed investing, here's a practical TLH routine:

  1. Review your taxable portfolio during market downturns — not during normal conditions.
  2. Identify positions with unrealized losses greater than $1,000 (smaller losses may not justify the transaction complexity).
  3. Confirm you haven't bought a substantially identical security in the past 30 days (including reinvested dividends — these count).
  4. Execute the sale and immediately (same day) buy the replacement fund.
  5. Set a calendar reminder for 31 days later if you want to swap back to the original fund.
  6. Track the cost basis carefully — your brokerage will do this, but verify it matches your records.

Tax-loss harvesting is one of the few investment strategies with a genuine, documented, repeatable edge — not from market prediction but from optimizing your tax liability. In a well-constructed taxable portfolio, it's not optional — it's standard practice.
