Tax-Loss Harvesting: Turn Losing Investments Into Tax Savings

January 30, 202612 min read

Tax-loss harvesting is one of the most powerful — and consistently underused — tax strategies available to investors. It requires no market predictions, no sophisticated financial planning, and is accessible to anyone with a taxable brokerage account. Done correctly, it can reduce your tax bill by thousands of dollars per year while keeping your investment strategy completely intact. Here is everything you need to know to implement it effectively.

What it is in plain English: When investments in your taxable account fall below their purchase price, you sell them to realize the loss. That loss offsets realized gains elsewhere, reducing your taxable income. You immediately reinvest the proceeds in a similar (but not identical) investment to maintain market exposure. Your portfolio barely changes. Your tax bill can drop significantly.

Why Tax-Loss Harvesting Works: The Tax Code Mechanics

The IRS allows investors to deduct realized capital losses against realized capital gains dollar-for-dollar. If you sell stock A for a $10,000 gain and stock B for a $10,000 loss in the same tax year, your net capital gain is $0 — and you owe no capital gains tax on either transaction.

Even better: if your capital losses exceed your capital gains in a given year, you can deduct up to $3,000 of net losses against ordinary income (wages, salary). Any remaining losses carry forward indefinitely to offset gains in future years. A $15,000 loss can offset $15,000 in gains — or $3,000/year of ordinary income for 5 years if there are no gains to offset.

The tax rates you're avoiding:

  • Long-term capital gains (assets held 12+ months): 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income. High earners also pay 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax, bringing the effective rate to 23.8%.
  • Short-term capital gains (held under 12 months): Taxed as ordinary income — up to 37% for top earners. Tax-loss harvesting is most valuable against short-term gains.

On a $50,000 realized gain, harvested losses can save a high-income investor $11,900 (at the 23.8% combined rate) in a single year. For most investors in the 15% long-term rate bracket, the same $50,000 in harvested losses saves $7,500. Not theoretical — real after-tax dollars.

The Wash-Sale Rule: The Critical Constraint

The IRS is not naive. The wash-sale rule (IRC Section 1091) prohibits claiming a loss on a security if you purchase a "substantially identical" security within 30 days before or after the sale — a 61-day wash-sale window centered on the transaction date.

If you trigger the wash-sale rule, the loss is disallowed (added to the cost basis of the replacement shares instead). No current-year tax benefit.

What counts as "substantially identical":

  • The exact same stock (selling AAPL and buying AAPL back within 30 days)
  • Call options on the same stock
  • The exact same mutual fund or ETF (selling SPY and buying SPY)

What does NOT count as substantially identical (safe replacements):

  • Different funds tracking different but similar indexes: selling VOO (S&P 500) and buying IVV (also S&P 500) IS substantially identical — same index. Selling VOO and buying VTI (total market) is likely safe, though some caution applies.
  • Selling an S&P 500 ETF and buying a total world fund (VT) — clearly different
  • Selling one tech company stock and buying a different tech company
  • Selling a bond ETF and buying a different bond ETF with different composition

Safe ETF swap pairs commonly used for tax-loss harvesting:

  • VOO (S&P 500, Vanguard) ↔ SCHB (Total Market, Schwab) — different index families, different compositions
  • VTI (Total Market, Vanguard) ↔ ITOT (Total Market, iShares) — some practitioners consider these too similar; use SCHB or FSKAX instead
  • VXUS (International ex-U.S.) ↔ IXUS (iShares equivalent) — different providers, same general exposure
  • BND (Aggregate Bonds, Vanguard) ↔ AGG (Aggregate Bonds, iShares) — effectively identical; most tax advisors consider these substantially identical. Use SCHZ or a Treasury-focused fund as the replacement.

Step-by-Step Implementation

Step 1: Monitor positions for unrealized losses. Review your taxable account holdings quarterly, and more frequently during market corrections. Identify positions showing unrealized losses — particularly those down more than 5%–10% from your cost basis.

Step 2: Calculate the tax benefit vs. transaction costs. Is the loss large enough to be worth the transaction? At modern zero-commission brokers, transaction costs are essentially zero, so almost any harvestable loss has positive expected value. Still, very small losses (under $500) may not be worth the administrative complexity.

Step 3: Execute the sale and immediately reinvest. Sell the loss position and immediately purchase your designated replacement security. Being out of the market even for a few days creates reinvestment timing risk — particularly in volatile markets where the biggest up-days often cluster with the biggest down-days.

Step 4: Calendar the wash-sale window. Note the date of your sale and set a reminder for 31 days later. After 31 days, you may repurchase the original security if desired. Until then, do not add to the original position through any account (including IRAs and spouse's accounts — the wash-sale rule applies household-wide).

Step 5: Track your cost basis meticulously. Your brokerage should track this automatically, but verify that the replacement shares' cost basis is correctly recorded. The "tax basis" column in your account should reflect what you actually paid for the replacement shares.

When Tax-Loss Harvesting Is Most Valuable

The strategy delivers the greatest benefit in specific circumstances:

  • High-income years with large realized gains: Selling a business, real estate, or other large assets triggers significant gains. Systematically harvested losses from your portfolio can offset a meaningful portion.
  • During market corrections: When portfolios fall 15%–30%, nearly every position shows a loss. This creates a large, temporary opportunity to harvest significant losses with minimal transaction cost.
  • Early in an investment portfolio's life: New investors with volatile individual stock positions have more harvesting opportunities than established portfolios with deeply appreciated holdings.
  • High tax brackets: The higher your marginal rate, the more valuable each dollar of harvested losses. At a 37% ordinary income rate, $3,000 of harvestable losses offsets $1,110 in taxes on ordinary income — vs. $450 at the 15% rate.

Common Tax-Loss Harvesting Mistakes

  • Triggering wash sales inadvertently: Automatic dividend reinvestment in the same security within the wash-sale window disallows the harvested loss. Turn off automatic DRIP (dividend reinvestment) for positions you may harvest.
  • Harvesting in tax-advantaged accounts: Tax-loss harvesting only applies to taxable accounts. Losses realized inside an IRA or 401(k) provide no tax benefit.
  • Ignoring the future tax consequence: Harvesting a loss lowers your cost basis in the replacement security. When you eventually sell that security for a gain, you'll owe more tax. Tax-loss harvesting is a tax deferral strategy, not pure tax elimination (unless you hold until death and benefit from the step-up in basis, or donate shares to charity).
  • Letting the tax tail wag the investment dog: Don't hold a fundamentally deteriorating investment just to avoid realizing a loss, or harvest a loss in an investment you genuinely want to hold long-term.

Automating Tax-Loss Harvesting: Robo-Advisors

Platforms like Betterment and Wealthfront automate daily tax-loss harvesting using sophisticated algorithms that monitor every position for harvestable opportunities. Their research suggests automated daily harvesting adds 0.5%–1.5% per year in after-tax alpha for taxable investors with moderate volatility portfolios — particularly in the first decade of account growth. For investors who don't want to manually implement the strategy, automated tax-loss harvesting through a robo-advisor is an efficient alternative worth evaluating.

The Bottom Line

Tax-loss harvesting is not a exotic hedge fund strategy — it is a straightforward, legal, and accessible technique for reducing your annual investment tax burden. The wash-sale rule is the critical constraint, but it is easily navigated with proper replacement security planning. Implemented consistently, particularly during market downturns, tax-loss harvesting can add meaningful after-tax value to your portfolio over a lifetime of investing.
