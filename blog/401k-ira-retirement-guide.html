401(k) vs IRA vs Roth IRA: The Complete Retirement Account Guide

January 8, 202616 min read

By WealthAxis Editorial Team

Our editorial team researches personal finance and investing topics using primary sources, regulatory guidance, and long-term market evidence to deliver clear, practical analysis.

Retirement accounts are among the most powerful wealth-building tools available to American investors — yet most people significantly under-utilize them. The difference between optimally using your available tax-advantaged accounts and leaving money on the table can easily exceed $500,000 in final retirement wealth. This guide covers every major retirement account type, the 2026 contribution limits, the tax mechanics of each, and the exact order in which you should fund them.

The contribution priority order: (1) 401(k) to employer match → (2) HSA if eligible → (3) Roth IRA (or Traditional IRA) to maximum → (4) 401(k) to annual maximum → (5) Taxable brokerage for anything beyond. This order maximizes tax advantages and free money at each step.

The 401(k): Your Most Accessible Retirement Account

The 401(k) is an employer-sponsored retirement plan available to employees of for-profit companies. Similar plans — the 403(b) for nonprofits and educational institutions, and the 457(b) for government employees — function essentially the same way.

2026 contribution limits: $23,500 for employees under 50. $31,000 for those 50+ (including a $7,500 catch-up contribution). These limits apply to employee contributions only — employer matching contributions are separate and don't count toward this ceiling.

Traditional 401(k) tax treatment: Contributions reduce your taxable income dollar-for-dollar in the year contributed. If you earn $90,000 and contribute $23,500, your taxable income for the year is $66,500. Growth inside the account is tax-deferred. Withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income. Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) begin at age 73.

Roth 401(k) tax treatment: Contributions are made with after-tax dollars (no current deduction). Growth and qualified withdrawals are completely tax-free. No RMDs during the account owner's lifetime (after the SECURE 2.0 Act eliminated Roth 401(k) RMDs). For investors who expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement than today, Roth 401(k) is superior.

The employer match: Free money you should never leave behind. If your employer matches 50% of contributions up to 6% of salary, and you earn $80,000, the match is worth up to $2,400 per year — a guaranteed 50% return on the first 6% of contributions before investments return a single dollar. At minimum, contribute enough to capture the full employer match before any other investment decision.

401(k) fund selection: Most 401(k) plans offer limited fund choices — often 15–30 options. Select the lowest-cost index funds available. Prioritize: a broad U.S. equity index fund (S&P 500 or total market), an international index fund if available, and a bond index fund. If your plan lacks low-cost index options, the S&P 500 fund with the lowest expense ratio is typically the default best choice.

The Traditional IRA: Tax-Deferred Flexibility

The Individual Retirement Account (IRA) is opened independently at a brokerage — not through an employer. This gives you complete control over fund selection, access to the full universe of ETFs and mutual funds, and the ability to implement strategies (like tax-loss harvesting coordination) unavailable in most 401(k) plans.

2026 contribution limits: $7,000 per individual ($8,000 if age 50+). These limits are shared between Traditional and Roth IRA — you can contribute $7,000 total across both, not $7,000 to each.

Deductibility income limits (2026, single filers): If you (or your spouse) have access to a workplace retirement plan, the deductibility of Traditional IRA contributions phases out between $79,000 and $89,000 of MAGI for single filers, and $126,000–$146,000 for married filing jointly. Above these limits, Traditional IRA contributions are non-deductible — making the Roth IRA (or the Backdoor Roth, discussed below) generally preferable.

Traditional IRA tax treatment: Deductible contributions reduce current-year taxable income. Growth is tax-deferred. Withdrawals in retirement are ordinary income. RMDs begin at 73.

When Traditional IRA beats Roth IRA: If you expect to be in a meaningfully lower tax bracket in retirement than today, the Traditional IRA's current deduction is more valuable than the Roth's future tax-free withdrawals. High-income earners in peak earning years often benefit from the Traditional IRA's immediate deduction.

The Roth IRA: The Most Powerful Account for Long-Term Wealth

The Roth IRA is, for most investors with long time horizons, the single most valuable account in the tax-advantaged universe. Its combination of tax-free growth, no RMDs, flexible contribution withdrawal rules, and estate planning advantages make it exceptional.

2026 income limits for direct contributions: Single filers: contributions phase out between $150,000–$165,000 MAGI. Married filing jointly: phase out between $236,000–$246,000 MAGI. Above these limits, direct Roth IRA contributions are not permitted.

Roth IRA tax treatment: Contributions are after-tax (no deduction). Growth is completely tax-free. Qualified withdrawals in retirement are tax-free. You can withdraw your contributions (not earnings) at any time without penalty or tax — this makes the Roth IRA also function as a flexible emergency fund of last resort.

Why the Roth IRA wins for most younger investors:

  • Tax-free growth over 20–40 years produces extraordinary tax savings on large final balances
  • No RMDs give maximum estate planning flexibility — the account can grow tax-free for your lifetime
  • Roth assets are incredibly valuable in a diversified "tax bucket" retirement strategy
  • Young investors often have temporarily low incomes (early career, graduate school), making current-year deductions less valuable and locking in lower tax rates through Roth contributions

The five-year rule: To withdraw Roth earnings tax-free, the account must be at least 5 years old AND you must be at least 59.5. This applies per account — open your Roth IRA as early as possible to start the 5-year clock, even if you can only contribute a small amount initially.

The Backdoor Roth IRA: For High Earners Above Income Limits

High earners above the Roth IRA income limits can still access Roth IRA benefits through the "Backdoor Roth" strategy:

  1. Make a non-deductible contribution to a Traditional IRA ($7,000 in 2026)
  2. Wait a few days for the funds to settle
  3. Convert the Traditional IRA balance to Roth IRA
  4. Report the conversion on Form 8606

Since the contribution was non-deductible (no tax deduction), the conversion triggers minimal tax — only on any small earnings that accumulated between contribution and conversion. Done promptly after contribution, the tax consequence is typically negligible.

The pro-rata rule: If you have existing pre-tax money in Traditional IRAs, the backdoor Roth becomes more complicated. The IRS aggregates all IRA balances when calculating the taxable portion of a conversion. High earners with existing pre-tax IRAs may want to explore rolling those balances into a 401(k) to clear the way for clean backdoor Roth contributions.

The HSA: The Rare Triple-Tax-Advantaged Account

The Health Savings Account (HSA) is available only to individuals enrolled in a High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP). If you qualify, it is arguably the most tax-advantaged account in the U.S. tax code — and many financial planners consider it the single best retirement savings vehicle available.

2026 contribution limits: $4,300 for self-only coverage; $8,550 for family coverage. An additional $1,000 catch-up for those 55+.

The triple tax advantage:

  • Contributions are pre-tax (reduce current taxable income)
  • Growth inside the account is tax-free
  • Withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free

No other account in the U.S. tax code offers all three. The 401(k) and Traditional IRA give you #1 and #2. The Roth IRA gives you #2 and #3. The HSA gives you all three simultaneously.

The HSA retirement strategy: Pay all current medical expenses out-of-pocket (if cash flow permits), saving receipts. Invest the HSA in index funds and let it compound tax-free for decades. After age 65, withdraw for medical expenses (still tax-free) or for any purpose (taxable as ordinary income, like a Traditional IRA). Given that average healthcare costs in retirement exceed $300,000 per couple, the HSA's tax-free withdrawal provision for medical expenses is exceptionally valuable.

The Self-Employed Retirement Accounts: Solo 401(k) and SEP-IRA

Freelancers, consultants, and business owners have access to retirement accounts with much higher contribution limits than employed individuals:

Solo 401(k): Available to self-employed individuals with no full-time employees (other than a spouse). In 2026: contribute up to $23,500 as "employee" + up to 25% of net self-employment income as "employer" contribution, with a combined maximum of $70,000. The Roth option is available in many Solo 401(k) plans. Loans are permitted. Best overall option for most self-employed individuals.

SEP-IRA: Simpler to establish and maintain than a Solo 401(k). Contribution limit: up to 25% of net self-employment income, maximum $70,000 in 2026. No Roth option. No loans. Excellent for very high earners who want simplicity and maximum deductibility.

Roth Conversions: Filling the Low-Income Years

A powerful strategy for retirees and those in early retirement: converting Traditional IRA or 401(k) balances to Roth during low-income years. If your income drops significantly in early retirement (common for FIRE practitioners and early retirees before Social Security begins), you may be in the 12% or even 0% marginal tax bracket. Converting pre-tax retirement funds to Roth during these years locks in a permanently low tax rate on those funds — reducing future RMDs and creating tax-free income for later life.

The Optimal Contribution Sequence

  1. 401(k) to employer match: Guaranteed immediate return of 50%–100%. Do this before everything else.
  2. HSA to maximum: If on an HDHP, this triple-tax-advantaged account deserves priority after capturing the employer match.
  3. Roth IRA to maximum: $7,000 in tax-free growth is exceptional for most investors. Backdoor Roth if above income limits.
  4. 401(k) to annual maximum: Max out the remaining 401(k) contribution room ($23,500 minus what you contributed in step 1).
  5. Taxable brokerage: After exhausting all tax-advantaged options, invest additional savings in a taxable account using tax-efficient ETFs.

The Bottom Line

Retirement accounts are legal tax shelters — and the U.S. tax code offers them in abundance. The investors who retire wealthy don't necessarily earn more than their peers; they consistently maximize their use of available tax-advantaged space over decades of compounding. Follow the contribution priority order, understand the tax characteristics of each account type, and implement Roth conversions strategically during low-income years.
