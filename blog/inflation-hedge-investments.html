The Best Inflation Hedges: How to Protect Your Portfolio in 2026

February 28, 202615 min read

Inflation is the slow tax that doesn't require a vote. At 3% annual inflation, a dollar loses half its purchasing power in just 24 years. At 6%, it takes only 12 years. Most people understand this intellectually — but far fewer take systematic action to protect their portfolios from inflation's relentless erosion.

The challenge is that not all inflation hedges work equally well, they don't all work at the same time, and some popular "inflation hedges" are more myth than reality. This guide examines seven of the most credible inflation-protection strategies, with data on how they've actually performed during inflationary periods.

Key distinction: An inflation hedge is an asset whose real return (return minus inflation) is positive or non-negative during inflationary periods. Many assets that "go up" during inflation still go up less than inflation — meaning you're losing purchasing power while feeling like you're making money.

1. Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS)

TIPS are U.S. government bonds whose principal is adjusted upward with CPI (Consumer Price Index) inflation. If you hold $10,000 of TIPS and inflation runs at 4%, the principal becomes $10,400 — and your interest payment is calculated on this new, higher principal. This makes TIPS a direct, mechanical inflation hedge with zero credit risk (backed by the U.S. government).

Real yield matters most: The "yield" quoted for TIPS is a real yield — already adjusted for inflation. A TIPS with a 1.5% real yield means you earn 1.5% above whatever inflation turns out to be. In recent years, real yields on TIPS have ranged from deeply negative (when demand was high) to meaningfully positive (when inflation surged and the Fed raised rates).

How to access TIPS: You can buy individual TIPS directly at TreasuryDirect.gov with no fees, or access them via ETFs like SCHP (Schwab U.S. TIPS ETF, 0.03% expense ratio) or TIP (iShares TIPS Bond ETF).

Limitations: TIPS protect against CPI-measured inflation, which may not perfectly match your personal inflation experience. They also lose value in real terms when real interest rates rise (even if nominal inflation is high) — as 2022 demonstrated, when TIPS fell 12% despite elevated inflation.

2. I-Bonds (U.S. Series I Savings Bonds)

I-Bonds are a unique inflation hedge available to U.S. investors directly from the Treasury. Their yield has two components: a fixed real rate (set at purchase and held for the life of the bond) plus a variable rate that adjusts every six months based on CPI. During the 2021–2022 inflation surge, I-Bonds were yielding over 9% annualized — the highest risk-free return available anywhere.

Key constraints: Maximum purchase of $10,000 per person per year (plus $5,000 via tax refund). Must be held at least 12 months. If redeemed within 5 years, you forfeit the last 3 months of interest. You must buy at TreasuryDirect.gov — they are not available on the secondary market or through brokerages.

Despite these limitations, I-Bonds represent an exceptional inflation hedge for the portion of your portfolio they can cover — particularly for money you won't need for 1–5 years.

3. Equities — Especially Dividend Growers and Pricing-Power Businesses

Common wisdom says stocks hedge inflation. The reality is more nuanced. Over very long periods (20+ years), broad equities have outpaced inflation significantly — the S&P 500 has delivered approximately 7% real annual returns. But over shorter inflationary episodes, stocks often struggle, particularly growth/tech stocks whose valuations depend on discounting distant future earnings.

Stocks that tend to outperform during inflation:

  • Companies with pricing power: Businesses that can raise prices without losing customers (luxury goods, consumer staples, certain software) can pass inflation to customers and protect margins. Think Coca-Cola, Procter & Gamble, Visa, Mastercard.
  • Dividend growth stocks: Companies consistently growing dividends are typically highly profitable, cash-generative businesses with durable competitive advantages — and growing dividends can offset inflation's impact on real income.
  • Energy sector: Energy companies are natural beneficiaries of commodity inflation — rising oil and gas prices directly increase revenues and profits.
  • Financial sector (selectively): Banks benefit from higher interest rates (wider net interest margins), though highly leveraged financials can suffer if inflation leads to credit stress.

4. Commodities

Commodities — oil, natural gas, agricultural products, industrial metals — are one of the most direct inflation hedges because they are literally the inputs whose rising prices cause inflation. When oil prices spike, that's both a cause and a measure of inflation — and commodity investors profit from the same price increase that hurts everyone else.

Historical evidence: Commodities have one of the strongest correlations with unexpected inflation of any asset class. During the 2021–2022 inflation surge, the Bloomberg Commodity Index returned approximately 40% — while the S&P 500 declined.

Practical access: Direct commodity ownership (barrels of oil, bushels of wheat) is impractical for most investors. Options include commodity ETFs (DJP, PDBC), commodity futures funds, or commodity-producing equities (ExxonMobil, Freeport-McMoRan, Archer-Daniels-Midland). Note that commodity futures ETFs suffer from "roll yield" drag that reduces returns over time — commodity-producing equities are often a more efficient vehicle for long-term investors.

Limitation: Commodities are extremely volatile, have no yield or intrinsic earning power, and can decline dramatically outside inflationary periods. A 5–10% allocation is generally recommended — not a core portfolio holding.

5. Real Estate (Direct and REITs)

Real estate has been one of the most reliable long-term inflation hedges for three reasons: (1) replacement cost inflation raises property values as building gets more expensive, (2) rents tend to rise with inflation (in most lease structures, particularly shorter-term residential leases), and (3) fixed-rate mortgage debt becomes cheaper in real terms as inflation reduces the real value of the outstanding balance.

Landlords with fixed 30-year mortgages are particularly well-positioned during inflation — their debt payment stays the same in nominal terms while rents and property values rise, dramatically compressing the real cost of their debt.

REIT performance during inflation: REITs have historically maintained or grown real returns during moderate inflation periods (2–4% CPI). During rapid, unexpected inflation spikes, REITs sometimes struggle short-term because rising interest rates increase their borrowing costs and compress valuation multiples. Longer-term (3+ years), they tend to recover and outperform as rents catch up.

6. Gold and Precious Metals

Gold is perhaps the most discussed inflation hedge — and one of the most misunderstood. The data on gold as an inflation hedge is decidedly mixed. Over very long periods (centuries), gold has maintained purchasing power. Over shorter, more relevant periods of 10–30 years, the correlation between gold returns and inflation has been inconsistent.

Gold significantly underperformed during the moderate inflation of the 1980s–2000s. It performed well during the 2000s and the 2020 crisis but poorly during the 2021–2022 inflation surge (a period when it "should" have performed well). This inconsistency reflects that gold prices are driven as much by real interest rates, dollar strength, and fear/uncertainty as by inflation itself.

The case for a small gold allocation: Despite its imperfect inflation-hedging record, gold has low correlation with stocks and bonds — making it a useful portfolio diversifier even if its inflation-protection qualities are overstated. A 5% allocation to gold (via GLD, IAU, or GLDM) is a reasonable hedge against tail-risk scenarios, even if it doesn't reliably track CPI.

7. Floating-Rate Bonds and Short-Duration Fixed Income

Traditional long-duration bonds are the worst-performing asset during inflationary periods — their fixed coupon payments become worth less in real terms, and rising interest rates crush their market value. The 2022 bond market bear market was the worst in modern history, with aggregate bond funds falling 13–15% — simultaneously with one of the worst equity years in decades.

The inflation-resistant fixed income alternatives:

  • Floating-rate loans/bonds: These instruments pay interest that resets periodically based on a benchmark rate (SOFR). As rates rise with inflation, the coupon payments increase. ETFs: FLOT, BKLN, SRLN.
  • Short-duration bonds (1–3 year maturities): Short-duration bonds mature quickly, allowing the proceeds to be reinvested at higher rates as they rise. Much less rate-sensitive than long-duration bonds.
  • Money market funds: During high inflation, money market funds yielding 4–5% provide a high-quality, liquid, inflation-competitive return without duration risk.

Building an Inflation-Resistant Portfolio

No single asset hedges inflation perfectly across all time periods and inflation environments. A practical portfolio approach:

  • Core equities (60–70%): U.S. + international index funds — long-term inflation outperformance
  • Real assets (10–15%): REIT ETF + commodity ETF — direct inflation sensitivity
  • Inflation-linked bonds (5–10%): TIPS or I-Bonds — mechanical CPI linkage
  • Short-duration fixed income (5–10%): Floating-rate funds or short bond funds
  • Gold (0–5%): Tail-risk insurance and low-correlation diversifier

The specific allocations depend on your time horizon, risk tolerance, and current inflation environment. Younger investors with decades ahead can rely more heavily on equities. Investors in or near retirement, who can't wait out a decade-long recovery, need more explicit inflation protection in their fixed income allocation.
