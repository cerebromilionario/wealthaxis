International Investing: Why and How to Diversify Beyond US Stocks

February 28, 202612 min read

By WealthAxis Editorial Team

Our editorial team researches personal finance and investing topics using primary sources, regulatory guidance, and long-term market evidence to deliver clear, practical analysis.

US investors have enjoyed extraordinary returns over the past 15 years — so extraordinary that many have questioned whether international diversification still makes sense. The S&P 500's outperformance vs. international developed markets from 2010 to 2021 was historically unusual. And precisely because of that dominance, US valuations entering 2026 are elevated relative to global peers in ways that historically precede periods of international outperformance.

Understanding global markets, how to access them efficiently, and what risks they introduce is essential for any investor building a durable long-term portfolio.

The global market reality: The US represents approximately 60–65% of global stock market capitalization — down from roughly 45% in 1990 as international markets have developed. The remaining 35–40% of global equity value exists outside the US in dozens of countries, across sectors and economic cycles that don't always move with American markets. Ignoring 40% of the world's investable equity universe is a choice that deserves deliberate justification.

The Case for International Diversification

Valuation argument: As of early 2026, US equities trade at approximately 20–22x trailing earnings (P/E). International developed markets trade at approximately 13–15x. Emerging markets at 11–13x. The US valuation premium is not unprecedented, but it does mean you're paying more for each dollar of current earnings globally when you concentrate in US equities.

Research shows that starting valuation is among the strongest predictors of 10-year forward returns. Markets starting at high valuations tend to deliver lower subsequent returns; markets starting at lower valuations tend to deliver higher returns. The current global valuation gap suggests a reasonable expectation of international markets outperforming US markets over the next decade — though timing is impossible to predict.

Economic cycle diversification: Different national economies don't always cycle together. When US growth is slow, European or Asian economies may be expanding. When US interest rates are high and compressing equities, markets in different rate environments may not face the same headwinds. International exposure provides genuine economic cycle diversification that purely domestic portfolios cannot access.

Currency diversification: Owning international stocks means owning assets denominated in foreign currencies. When the US dollar weakens (as it has periodically — it fell substantially against major currencies in 2002–2007 and again in parts of 2020), international stocks generate additional returns for US investors. Currency diversification is a real, quantifiable benefit.

Sector exposure: The US market is heavily weighted toward technology and financials. International developed markets have higher weights in industrials, energy, healthcare, and consumer staples. Emerging markets provide access to high-growth economies in consumer goods, financials, and resources. The sector composition of global markets is genuinely different from the US — providing sector diversification beyond what's achievable domestically.

Developed Markets vs. Emerging Markets

Developed Markets include the US, UK, Japan, Germany, France, Canada, Australia, Switzerland, the Netherlands, Sweden, and approximately 20 other high-income countries with deep, liquid capital markets and strong rule of law. The MSCI EAFE Index (Europe, Australasia, Far East) is the standard benchmark for developed market international exposure, excluding the US.

Developed markets are characterized by: stable currencies, reliable accounting standards, strong shareholder protections, liquid markets with tight bid-ask spreads, and lower political risk. Returns are generally lower-volatility than emerging markets and correlate more closely with US equities (but still provide meaningful diversification).

Japan deserves special mention: the world's third-largest economy with a vibrant corporate sector has been experiencing a remarkable corporate governance transformation. The Tokyo Stock Exchange's 2023 "Prime Market" reform pressure on companies to improve capital efficiency has driven significant stock buybacks and dividend increases. Japanese equities entered 2026 at approximately 15x earnings — historically cheap for a developed market economy — with corporate governance tailwinds. The Nikkei 225 reached all-time highs in 2024 for the first time since 1989.

Emerging Markets (EM) include China, India, Brazil, Taiwan, South Korea, Saudi Arabia, Indonesia, and approximately 20 other developing economies. The MSCI Emerging Markets Index captures these markets.

EM characteristics: higher long-term growth potential (GDP growth in India, Indonesia, and Vietnam has consistently exceeded developed market rates), significantly higher volatility, currency risk (often more dramatic), political risk (nationalization, regulatory changes, geopolitical tension), accounting and governance risk (corporate disclosure standards vary widely), and liquidity risk in smaller markets.

The EM story of the past decade has been complicated by China, which represents approximately 25–30% of the MSCI EM Index. Chinese equities experienced dramatic regulatory intervention in 2021 (particularly in technology and education sectors), geopolitical tensions around Taiwan, and economic headwinds from a property sector crisis. The MSCI EM Index significantly underperformed US equities largely because of China's underperformance. India has been a notable bright spot — the third-largest market in MSCI EM, with strong economic growth and equity returns.

Key International Risks

Currency Risk: When you buy a Japanese stock through a US brokerage, you own yen-denominated assets. If the Japanese yen weakens against the US dollar, your returns are reduced even if the stock price rises in yen terms. Currency effects can add or subtract 3–8% annually from international returns, depending on currency movements.

Currency hedging is available (through hedged ETFs like DBEF — Deutsche Bank MSCI EAFE Currency Hedged) and eliminates currency risk by using forwards contracts. Hedged international ETFs make sense for investors who want pure equity exposure without currency fluctuation. Unhedged international ETFs provide the currency diversification benefit but accept currency volatility.

Research suggests: for long-term investors, unhedged exposure provides the currency diversification benefit that's part of the argument for international investing. For shorter-term allocations or in periods of strong dollar expectations, hedging makes more sense. Many investors simply split the difference — half hedged, half unhedged.

Political and Regulatory Risk: Foreign governments can nationalize companies, impose capital controls, change tax treatment of foreign investors, or create regulatory uncertainty that impairs business value. Russia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022 rendered Russian equities essentially worthless for Western investors, as they were suspended from trading globally. This extreme scenario is rare but illustrates the tail risk of concentration in politically unstable markets.

Diversification across countries mitigates this risk substantially. A total international fund with exposure to 40+ countries means a catastrophic event in any one country is a modest portfolio impact rather than a devastating one.

Accounting and Governance Differences: Financial reporting standards differ internationally (IFRS vs. US GAAP). Corporate governance practices — board composition, minority shareholder protections, disclosure requirements — vary significantly. Generally speaking: UK, Europe, Canada, and Australia have very high standards; Japan is improving; emerging markets are more variable, with some countries (India, Taiwan, South Korea) having strong standards and others (some frontier markets) having significant risks.

The Best International ETFs for 2026

Total International (Developed + Emerging):

  • VXUS — Vanguard Total International Stock ETF (0.07%) — The gold standard for total international exposure. ~7,700 stocks across developed and emerging markets. Market-cap weighted.
  • IXUS — iShares Core MSCI Total International (0.07%) — Nearly identical to VXUS, from BlackRock.

Developed Markets Only (EAFE):

  • VEA — Vanguard FTSE Developed Markets ETF (0.05%) — Low-cost access to 3,700+ stocks in Europe, Japan, Australia, and other developed markets.
  • EFA — iShares MSCI EAFE (0.32%) — The oldest and most liquid EAFE ETF but higher cost than VEA.
  • SPDW — SPDR Portfolio Developed World ex-US (0.04%) — Very low cost developed markets exposure.

Emerging Markets Only:

  • VWO — Vanguard FTSE Emerging Markets ETF (0.08%) — Broad EM exposure including South Korea (which MSCI classifies as emerging).
  • IEMG — iShares Core MSCI Emerging Markets (0.09%) — Comparable to VWO from BlackRock.
  • SCHE — Schwab Emerging Markets Equity ETF (0.11%) — Schwab's competitive EM option.

Single-Country or Region Funds:

  • EWJ — iShares MSCI Japan (0.50%) — For targeted Japan exposure
  • INDA — iShares MSCI India (0.65%) — India exposure for higher-conviction EM investors
  • EWG — iShares MSCI Germany (0.50%) — European industrial economy exposure

How Much International Allocation Is Optimal?

There's no universally "correct" international allocation, but research and practitioner consensus cluster around certain ranges:

  • Global market-cap weight: ~40% international (matches actual global equity capitalization). Endorsed by Vanguard's research as the neutral, unbiased starting point.
  • Common practitioner range: 20–40% of total equity allocation in international stocks. Most financial advisors land in this range for diversified investors.
  • Home-bias adjusted: Many investors hold 20–30% international, accepting some home bias for practical reasons (familiarity, currency simplicity, tax treatment).

The most important principle: have some meaningful international exposure. A portfolio of 100% US stocks is a significant concentration bet on one country's equity market — even if that country happens to be the world's largest economy. The past 15 years rewarded this bet; there's no guarantee the next 15 will.

For simplicity: if you own VT (Vanguard Total World Stock ETF, 0.07%), you own approximately 60% US and 40% international at market-cap weight — exactly matching the global market portfolio in a single ticker. This is the easiest implementation of a globally diversified equity portfolio.
