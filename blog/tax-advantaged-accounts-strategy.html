Tax-Advantaged Accounts: How to Stack HSA, 401(k), and IRA for Maximum Wealth

March 4, 202613 min read

By WealthAxis Editorial Team

Our editorial team researches personal finance and investing topics using primary sources, regulatory guidance, and long-term market evidence to deliver clear, practical analysis.

The US tax code, for all its complexity, contains remarkable gifts for disciplined investors: multiple account types that shelter investment growth from taxation, each with different rules, limits, and optimal use cases. Most Americans use one or two of these accounts. Sophisticated investors stack them systematically, potentially sheltering hundreds of thousands of dollars from taxation over a career.

This guide covers the optimal contribution order, the underutilized HSA strategy, and how to coordinate multiple account types for maximum after-tax wealth accumulation.

The foundational principle: Every dollar of investment return kept from the tax authority is a dollar that continues compounding in your portfolio. The difference between a 7% nominal return and a 7% after-tax return on $500,000 over 30 years is enormous. Tax-advantaged accounts are the mechanism for keeping more of what you earn from investing.

The Account Types: A Quick Reference

Before the strategy, a crisp summary of each major account type in 2026:

  • 401(k)/403(b) Traditional: Pre-tax contributions, tax-deferred growth, taxed on withdrawal. Limit: $23,500 + $7,500 catch-up (50+) or $11,250 catch-up (60–63 SECURE 2.0).
  • 401(k)/403(b) Roth: After-tax contributions, tax-free growth and withdrawals. Same limits as traditional.
  • Traditional IRA: Pre-tax (deductibility based on income/plan access), tax-deferred growth, taxed on withdrawal. Limit: $7,000 + $1,000 catch-up (50+).
  • Roth IRA: After-tax contributions, tax-free growth and withdrawals, no RMDs. Same limits as Traditional IRA. Income limits apply for direct contributions.
  • HSA (Health Savings Account): Triple tax advantage — pre-tax contributions, tax-free growth, tax-free qualified withdrawals. Limit: $4,300 single / $8,550 family in 2026 + $1,000 catch-up (55+). Requires HDHP enrollment.
  • 529 Plan: Education savings — after-tax contributions, tax-free growth, tax-free withdrawals for qualified education expenses. No federal limit but gift tax exclusion applies.
  • Taxable Brokerage: No tax advantages but no limits, no restrictions, no contribution limits.

The Optimal Contribution Order

The order in which you fill these accounts matters because each step has a different return guarantee or advantage:

Step 1 — 401(k) up to employer match

An employer match is a 50–100% guaranteed instant return. No investment can reliably match this. If your employer matches 50% of contributions up to 6% of salary, contribute exactly 6% before doing anything else. On a $100,000 salary, 6% = $6,000 contribution + $3,000 employer match = 50% instant return. This always comes first, regardless of your 401(k)'s fund quality.

Step 2 — HSA (if you have an HSA-eligible high-deductible health plan)

The HSA is the only account with a triple tax advantage: contributions are pre-tax (or deductible), growth is tax-free, and withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free. No other account provides all three. This makes the HSA more tax-advantaged per dollar than either a 401(k) or Roth IRA.

Max your HSA before your IRA. The 2026 limits ($4,300 single, $8,550 family) are smaller than retirement accounts, but the triple advantage makes each dollar more valuable. More on the HSA investment strategy below.

Step 3 — Roth IRA (for most people under 40)

After the employer match and HSA, max the Roth IRA ($7,000) if your income is within limits (phase-out at $150,000 single, $236,000 married). The Roth's tax-free growth advantage is most powerful when you have decades of compounding ahead. Roth also has no RMDs and more flexibility than traditional accounts.

If your income exceeds Roth limits, use the backdoor Roth (contribute to a non-deductible traditional IRA, then immediately convert to Roth). This legal workaround is available regardless of income.

Step 4 — Max 401(k) employee contributions

Return to your 401(k) and increase contributions to the $23,500 maximum. Even if your plan has mediocre fund options, the tax deferral is still extremely valuable — particularly in high tax brackets. An investor in the 32% bracket who contributes $23,500 pre-tax saves $7,520 in immediate federal income taxes.

In this step, decide traditional vs. Roth based on your tax bracket (see 401(k) guide for details). Many investors contribute traditional in peak earning years and Roth when income is lower.

Step 5 — Mega Backdoor Roth (if your plan allows)

If your 401(k) plan allows after-tax contributions (beyond the $23,500 pre-tax limit) and allows either in-plan Roth conversion or in-service distributions, you can contribute up to the full $70,000 415 limit — converting after-tax contributions immediately to Roth status. This allows $46,500 of additional Roth contributions annually beyond the standard limits.

This strategy requires checking your Summary Plan Description (SPD) for "after-tax contributions" and "in-plan conversions." Only about 20% of plans offer this, but it's transformative for high earners when available.

Step 6 — 529 Plan (if you have children or plan to)

529 plans are after-tax contributions with tax-free growth and withdrawals for qualified education expenses (tuition, fees, books, room and board at qualifying institutions). Most states offer a state income tax deduction for contributions to their plan.

SECURE 2.0 (2024 change) allows rolling unused 529 balances into the beneficiary's Roth IRA (up to $35,000 lifetime limit, subject to annual Roth IRA contribution limits), reducing overfunding risk dramatically.

Step 7 — Taxable Brokerage

After exhausting all tax-advantaged options, a taxable brokerage account is the next destination for investments. Tax-efficient fund selection (index ETFs with low turnover) and tax-loss harvesting can meaningfully reduce the tax drag in taxable accounts.

The HSA as a Stealth Retirement Account: The Advanced Strategy

Most people use their HSA as a medical expense savings account — depositing money and spending it on qualified medical costs. This is a massive underutilization of the account's potential.

The optimal HSA strategy for investors with other cash reserves:

  1. Max HSA contributions annually
  2. Pay all medical expenses out-of-pocket from regular cash flow — do NOT use HSA funds
  3. Keep all medical expense receipts (indefinitely — there's no time limit on HSA reimbursements)
  4. Invest all HSA funds in low-cost index ETFs (most HSA custodians now allow investment of funds above a cash threshold)
  5. Let the balance compound for decades
  6. In retirement, submit all the historical medical receipts for tax-free reimbursements

This turns the HSA into an additional tax-advantaged retirement account with a unique benefit: after age 65, HSA funds can be withdrawn for any reason (like a traditional IRA — taxed but no penalty), or for medical expenses tax-free. The receipts you saved from decades of out-of-pocket medical spending become a pool of tax-free withdrawals.

Example: $8,550/year family HSA contributions for 20 years at 8% return = approximately $420,000. If $150,000 in out-of-pocket medical expenses were paid over those 20 years (and receipts saved), you can reimburse yourself $150,000 tax-free immediately. The remaining $270,000 withdraws for any purpose at ordinary income rates (or continues to grow).

Asset Location: What Goes Where

Tax-advantaged accounts aren't just about contribution limits — where you hold each investment type matters enormously.

Best assets for Roth IRA (tax-free growth):

  • High-expected-return assets (small-cap equities, emerging markets, REITs)
  • High-dividend stocks and ETFs
  • Assets you plan to hold for decades without touching

Best assets for Traditional 401(k)/IRA (tax-deferred):

  • High-turnover actively managed funds (capital gains deferred)
  • REITs (high ordinary income distributions sheltered)
  • Bond funds (ordinary interest income deferred)

Best assets for Taxable Brokerage:

  • Buy-and-hold index ETFs (low turnover, mostly qualified dividends, tax-efficient)
  • Municipal bonds (tax-exempt income)
  • Tax-managed funds
  • Individual stocks (control over timing of gains realization)

Asset location optimization can add 0.2–0.5% to after-tax returns annually without any change to underlying investments — purely from where you hold them. Over decades, this compounds into significant additional wealth.

The Coordination Checklist

Annual questions to optimize your tax-advantaged account strategy:

  • Am I capturing my full employer match? (If not, adjust 401k contribution immediately)
  • Am I maxing my HSA? (If on an HDHP, this should always be maxed first)
  • Am I maxing my Roth IRA or traditional IRA? (Or backdoor Roth if over income limits)
  • Am I maxing my 401(k) beyond the match?
  • Does my plan allow mega backdoor Roth contributions? (Check SPD if unsure)
  • Are my assets in the right account types for tax efficiency?
  • Should I do Roth conversions this year? (Low-income years, before Social Security, after retirement)

The systematic use of all available tax-advantaged accounts is one of the highest-return activities available to any investor. The equivalent annual return from tax optimization — compounded over a career — dwarfs what most active investment strategies generate. It requires no stock picking, no market timing, and no exceptional financial sophistication — just consistent, disciplined execution of the account hierarchy year after year.
